Topic 6 — Financial Ratios

All questions covered · Try it first · Then reveal the answer

All the Formulas You Need

Profitability Higher = Better

Gross Profit Margin %
Gross Profit ÷ Sales × 100
Profit before overheads. Shows if pricing/cost of goods is healthy.
Net Profit Margin %
Net Profit ÷ Sales × 100
Profit after ALL costs. If much lower than gross margin, overheads are a problem.
ROCE %
Operating Profit ÷ Capital Employed × 100
Capital Employed = Total Assets − Current Liabilities. How well is the business using its capital?

Liquidity Can the business pay its short-term bills?

Current Ratio
Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities
Ideal: ~2:1. Below 1 = danger zone. Too high = cash sitting idle.
Quick (Acid Test) Ratio
(Current Assets − Stock) ÷ Current Liabilities
Removes stock (slowest asset to convert to cash). Ideal: ~1:1.

Efficiency How well are assets and money being used?

Asset Turnover
Sales ÷ Total Assets
Revenue per £1 of assets. Higher = more efficient use of assets.
Trade Payable Days
(Trade Payables ÷ Cost of Sales) × 365
How long the business takes to pay its suppliers.
Trade Receivable Days
(Trade Receivables ÷ Sales) × 365
How long customers take to pay. Lower = collecting cash faster.
Inventory Days
((Opening + Closing Stock) ÷ 2 ÷ Cost of Sales) × 365
How long stock sits before being sold. Lower = moving faster.
Golden rule: A ratio on its own means nothing. Always compare it to the previous year OR to a competitor to say something useful.

📉 Control Overhead Costs

Gross margin jumped to 69% but net margin fell to 12.92% — indirect costs are the problem. Do a full cost audit, renegotiate supplier contracts, and cut unnecessary overheads.

Long Term

🏗️ Improve Asset Utilisation

📝 Overall Summary

Gold Coast improved its gross profitability in 2023 — a genuine positive. But net profit is down, liquidity has weakened sharply (acid test fell from 3.48 to 1.25), stock is barely moving, and assets are generating far less revenue. The business must tackle its inventory problem and overhead costs urgently before they create a serious cash flow crisis.
